How to Travel from Kathmandu to Lhasa Overland

Home / Travel Blog / Blog Details

The journey from Kathmandu to Lhasa overland is one of the most epic road trips in the world. This route takes you through the heart of the Himalayas, crossing high-altitude passes, remote Tibetan villages, and some of the most breathtaking landscapes on Earth. Whether you're an adventure seeker, a culture enthusiast, or simply looking for an unforgettable travel experience, this guide will walk you through everything you need to know to make this trip a reality.

Why Choose the Overland Route?

Flying from Kathmandu to Lhasa is quick, but the overland journey offers something far more rewarding: an immersive experience through the rugged beauty of the Himalayas and the Tibetan Plateau. Here’s why you should consider taking the long road:

  • Stunning Scenery: From lush valleys to barren high-altitude deserts, the landscapes are constantly changing.
  • Cultural Immersion: You’ll pass through Tibetan Buddhist villages, ancient monasteries, and nomadic settlements.
  • Adventure Factor: Crossing the Friendship Highway and the Gyirong Border is an experience in itself.
  • Acclimatization Benefits: Traveling overland helps your body adjust gradually to the high altitude of Tibet.

The Best Time to Travel

Timing is crucial for this journey. The ideal months are April to June and September to October, when the weather is stable, and the roads are clear.

  • Avoid July & August: Monsoon rains can cause landslides, making the roads dangerous.
  • Winter Travel (Nov-Mar): Possible but extremely cold, with some mountain passes closed due to snow.

Required Permits and Documents

Traveling to Tibet requires special permits, and the overland route adds extra layers of bureaucracy. Here’s what you need:

1. Chinese Group Visa

If you’re entering Tibet from Nepal, you’ll need a Chinese Group Visa, which must be arranged through a registered travel agency in Kathmandu.

2. Tibet Travel Permit (TTP)

This is mandatory for all foreign travelers entering Tibet. Your tour operator will apply for it on your behalf.

3. Alien’s Travel Permit (ATP)

Required for travel outside Lhasa, including the overland route.

4. Border Pass (Gyirong Port)

Since you’re crossing from Nepal into Tibet, you’ll need a border pass issued by the Chinese authorities.

Pro Tip: Start the permit process at least 4-6 weeks before your trip to avoid delays.

The Overland Route: Kathmandu to Lhasa

The most common overland route follows the Friendship Highway (China-Nepal Highway), passing through Gyirong (the border town) and then onward to Lhasa. Here’s a breakdown of the journey:

Stage 1: Kathmandu to Gyirong (Nepal-China Border)

Day 1: Kathmandu to Syabrubesi (Drive: 7-8 hours)

  • The journey begins with a scenic drive through Nepal’s hills, passing terraced fields and traditional villages.
  • Overnight in Syabrubesi, the last major stop before the border.

Day 2: Syabrubesi to Gyirong (Drive: 4-5 hours)

  • Cross the Rasuwa Gadhi border into Tibet.
  • After immigration formalities, you’ll enter Gyirong, a lush valley town known as the "Switzerland of Tibet."

Stage 2: Gyirong to Lhasa

Day 3: Gyirong to Tingri (Drive: 6-7 hours)

  • The road climbs into the high-altitude Tibetan Plateau.
  • Stop at Pekutso Lake, a stunning turquoise lake surrounded by snow-capped peaks.
  • Overnight in Tingri (4,300m), a small town with incredible views of Mount Everest (North Face).

Day 4: Tingri to Shigatse (Drive: 5-6 hours)

  • Cross the Gyangtse La Pass (5,220m) and visit Pelkor Monastery and the Kumbum Stupa.
  • Arrive in Shigatse, Tibet’s second-largest city, home to the famous Tashilhunpo Monastery.

Day 5: Shigatse to Lhasa (Drive: 6-7 hours)

  • Follow the Yarlung Tsangpo River, stopping at small Tibetan villages.
  • Arrive in Lhasa, where the Potala Palace awaits as the grand finale of your journey.

Transportation Options

By Private Land Cruiser (Recommended)

  • Most travelers book a 4x4 Land Cruiser with a driver and guide through a tour agency.
  • Comfortable, reliable, and ideal for high-altitude travel.

By Public Bus (Budget Option)

  • There are occasional buses from Kathmandu to Lhasa, but they’re less flexible and require multiple transfers.
  • Not recommended due to limited comfort and permit complications.

Altitude Sickness: How to Stay Safe

Since much of the journey is above 4,000m, altitude sickness is a real concern. Here’s how to minimize risks:

  • Acclimatize Properly: Spend a few days in Kathmandu (1,400m) before ascending.
  • Stay Hydrated: Drink plenty of water and avoid alcohol.
  • Take It Slow: Don’t rush—allow your body to adjust.
  • Carry Medication: Diamox (acetazolamide) can help prevent symptoms.

Must-Visit Stops Along the Way

1. Gyirong Valley

  • A hidden gem with dense forests, waterfalls, and a mild climate—rare in Tibet!

2. Rongbuk Monastery & Everest Base Camp

  • The world’s highest monastery with jaw-dropping views of Mount Everest.

3. Yamdrok Lake

  • One of Tibet’s most sacred lakes, famous for its unreal shades of blue.

4. The Potala Palace

  • The iconic symbol of Lhasa and a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Budgeting for the Trip

Here’s a rough estimate of costs (per person):

  • Permits & Tour Package: $800-$1,500 (depending on group size and inclusions)
  • Transport (Private Jeep): $200-$400 (shared among group members)
  • Accommodation: $20-$50 per night (mid-range guesthouses)
  • Food & Miscellaneous: $15-$30 per day

Note: Independent travel is nearly impossible due to permit restrictions, so booking through a tour operator is necessary.

Final Tips for a Smooth Journey

  • Pack Warm Layers: Temperatures can drop drastically, especially at night.
  • Bring Snacks: Food options in remote areas are limited.
  • Respect Local Customs: Tibet has strict cultural norms—always ask before taking photos of people or religious sites.
  • Stay Flexible: Road conditions and weather can cause delays.

This overland adventure is not just a trip—it’s a life-changing experience. The raw beauty of the Himalayas, the spiritual depth of Tibetan culture, and the thrill of crossing one of the world’s most legendary routes make it a journey you’ll never forget.
